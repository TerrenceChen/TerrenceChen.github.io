**Constructing Partisan Governing Repertoires: The Making of Fiscally Responsible Democrats Since the 1970s**

*Dissertation Project*

**Question**: Why did fiscal responsibility become a durable component of Democratic political identity precisely when Republicans were moving away from it?

Combining computational text analysis of more than 8 million congressional speeches with archival evidence, I show that fiscal responsibility persisted within the Democratic Party not because all Democrats became neoliberals, but because liberal Democrats successfully articulated a version of fiscal responsibility that complemented, rather than contradicted, commitments to social protection and government activism. Moreover, fiscal responsibility served important political functions: it bridged different factions within the Democratic Party and enabled Democrats to criticize Republican governing records. I first show how these conditions for the endurance of fiscal responsibility emerged during the 1970s and 1980s. I then examine how these mechanisms were mobilized in two pivotal legislative episodes: the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1993 and the Statutory Pay-As-You-Go (PAYGO) Act of 2010. 



**Tracing Politicization in U.S. Public Discourse, 1980-2024** 

*Under Review*

**Question**: How has American public discourse become more politicized?

This project develops a new measure of discursive politicization using word embedding models trained on 1.35 million newspaper articles. Analyzing 131 issue areas between 1980 and 2024, I find that politicization has increased substantially over the past four decades and expanded beyond traditional political issues into many areas of social life. The perceived center of political conflict has also shifted, with race and immigration, gender and family, and legal institutions becoming more politicized than economic issues.
